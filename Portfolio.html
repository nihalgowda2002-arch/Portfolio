Fixed Income · Time Series · Machine Learning Independent project

Yield Curve Construction & Forecasting

A self-contained Python library that builds, models and forecasts the U.S. Treasury term structure — and turns those forecasts into a tradeable duration-timing strategy.

The library fits both the Nelson–Siegel and Nelson–Siegel–Svensson curves by concentrated least squares (NSS reaching 3.6 bps RMSE versus 4.3 bps for NS), bootstraps an arbitrage-consistent discount curve, and prices bonds, swaps, FRAs and Black–76 caplets with full duration, convexity and DV01 risk measures.

Forecasting is built from the ground up: a Kalman filter and RTS smoother in pure NumPy, a Dynamic Nelson–Siegel state-space model estimated by Kalman maximum-likelihood, plus VAR, ARIMA and a from-scratch LSTM (back-propagation through time, Adam optimiser). Crucially, every model is judged against an honest random-walk benchmark, then fed into duration-timing and 2s10s curve backtests.

Computational Finance: Pricing Engines

Three numerical option-pricing engines, each built independently and cross-validated against a closed-form solution to prove it is correct.

The first engine is a moment-matched binomial tree whose European price converges to within pennies of Black–Scholes. The second solves the CIR bond pricing PDE with a backward-Euler finite-difference scheme on a sparse tridiagonal system — pre-factorised once with an LU decomposition for speed — and agrees with the analytic price to four decimal places.

The third prices a barrier option by Monte Carlo, combining antithetic variates with a Black–Scholes control variate so that the 95% confidence interval collapses to roughly a third of the target tolerance. The work also includes a least-squares calibration exercise and a barrier-exceedance probability study.

Portfolio Construction · Optimisation Independent project

Portfolio Optimisation & Black–Litterman

A Modern Portfolio Theory pipeline that runs from raw data to a final allocation, then extends the classical approach with the Black–Litterman model.

Across a ten-asset, multi-sector universe the pipeline estimates expected returns, covariance and correlation, then solves for the maximum-Sharpe and minimum-variance portfolios with SciPy's SLSQP optimiser, tracing the full efficient frontier and capital market line.

A quarterly walk-forward backtest then re-optimises out of sample and reports Sharpe, Sortino, Calmar and maximum drawdown against an equal-weight benchmark. Finally, the Black–Litterman model blends reverse-optimised equilibrium returns with subjective views to produce stable posterior weights that avoid the corner solutions classical mean–variance is prone to.

Corporate Finance · Governance · Risk MSc group project

The Collapse of Silicon Valley Bank

An agency-theory analysis of the 2023 Silicon Valley Bank failure, and what it teaches about incentive design and financial regulation.

The report argues that an equity-heavy executive pay structure (roughly 60–75% of compensation, with no risk adjustment or clawbacks) rewarded management for taking on unhedged interest-rate and duration risk. That incentive problem was compounded by weak risk oversight — an eight-month vacancy in the Chief Risk Officer role and limited risk expertise on the board.

It situates the failure within the EGRRCPA deregulation that raised the enhanced-supervision threshold, and draws out concrete lessons on risk-sensitive pay, deposit-insurance design and disclosure. Delivered as a co-authored group report for the Corporate Finance & Governance module.

Dissertation · In Progress

Stochastic Volatility: Hull–White & Heston

Developing the theory and implementation of diffusion-based stochastic-volatility models that capture the volatility smile the Black–Scholes model cannot.

The dissertation studies two models in which volatility itself is a diffusion driven by a second Wiener process. This makes the market incomplete, so calibration to observed prices is what pins down the risk-neutral measure. The mathematical spine runs from Itô calculus through Girsanov's theorem to a generic stochastic-volatility pricing PDE.

From there it derives the Hull–White conditioning result — a Black–Scholes-style closed form obtained by integrating over the variance path — and the Heston model's pricing PDEs and semi-analytic solution. The applied component calibrates these models to market implied volatilities and reproduces the volatility smile and the fat tails seen in real option markets.
